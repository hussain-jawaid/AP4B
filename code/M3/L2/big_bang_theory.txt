The Big Bang Theory is the prevailing cosmological model explaining the existence of the observable universe from the earliest known periods through its subsequent large-scale evolution.

Key Concepts
The Beginning: The theory suggests that about 13.8 billion years ago, the entire universe was concentrated in a single point of infinite density and heat called a singularity.
Expansion, Not an Explosion: Despite the name, the Big Bang was not a conventional explosion in space. Instead, it was the rapid expansion of space itself, which caused the universe to cool and allow for the formation of matter.
Formation of Matter: As the universe cooled in the first few minutes, subatomic particles formed, followed by the simplest elements (mostly hydrogen and helium). Millions of years later, gravity pulled these gases together to form the first stars and galaxies.
Core Evidence
Scientists support this theory based on several major observations:

Redshift of Galaxies: Observations by Edwin Hubble showed that galaxies are moving away from us, indicating the universe is still expanding.
Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB): This is the "afterglow" of the Big Bang—faint cosmic radiation that fills all of space and was emitted when the universe first became transparent to light.
Abundance of Elements: The measured amounts of light elements (hydrogen and helium) in the universe match the predictions made by Big Bang nucleosynthesis.
